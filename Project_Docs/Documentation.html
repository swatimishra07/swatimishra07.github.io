US Energy Information Administration Data Visualization Document

Introduction

This webpage is used to visualize the data provided by US Energy Information Administration of the various countries in the world over the course of 33 years. This page has been developed as part of the individual project for Data visualization course INFO-H590 at IU School of Informatics and Computing.

The Salient features of the visualization are:

  • One can view trends in production and consumption of any energy of any country over time.
  • One can view any energy production or consumption, for any country , using a drop down menu
  • One can chose between the bar chart and line chart for data visualization.
  • One can chose to view multiple countries in form of line-chart or bar-chart, and compare the two or more energy trends for any country
  • One can also chose to view multiple energies in form of line-chart or bar-chart, and compare the energy trends for two or more countries
  • The charts are labeled for convenience.
  • The scatter pot is used to describe the trend in energies for all countries for a given year. It can be used for displaying how the energy is being consumed and produced and effecting other variables.
  • One can choose more than one energy type and view the combined scatter plot.
  • The visualization is simple to keep away distractions.

    • Design Process

    Since the data was huge and the scope of development was immense, I felt the safest strategy would be to develop from bottom to top. What this means is to start developing basic plots with only 1 variable plotted against time. And start building over it. I began by plotting one type of energy against time for one country. Once both bar charts and line charts were complete, then I picked more countries and started plotting them in comparison to one another. After several countries, it turned out that the data got too cluttered to make sense of, and I found logical to a put a restriction on how many countries can be plotted on bar and line charts. Hence, one can view maximum of four countries to compare against one another. The next step was to plot two or more energies for a single country. The same maximum limit for four energies is applied herr to reduce clutter.

    Once the ground work was laid, the next task was of designing the layout of the visualization. Once I had made the design choices, I had to integrate the bits and pieces of all the charts and fit into a single page. This task was messy, since it resulted into a lot of bugs cropping up while testing. It consumed a lot of time. However, if I compare from top to bottom approach, I feel it was comparatively less complex. Since the charts were all working fine, this step mainly involved giving a lot of flexibility to the user with simplicity.

    Design Choices

    The moment I realized we had to work with huge numbers and many variables, I had decided to keep the visualization as simple as possible.

    • Working with large numbers is a complex task for human mind and hence any distractions can result in misinterpretation of data. Hence the page layout is has been intentionally kept without any fancy details and clutter.
    • The drop down menu was a natural choice over any other text input mechanism owing to large number of variables available.
    • The click button to clear a choice of country added is to account for any change of county choice while displaying multiple countries
    • Also to provide feedback on what countries the user is going to view, the names of selected countries are displayed alongside the choice button. The same are displayed using graph legends.
    • The selection menu is divided into two separate sections, one for the scatter plot and the other for line/Bar Charts This was done intentionally to ease out what is input is required to view what fields
    • The Scatter plot was intended to give details on emerging trends in energy, and to highlight the influence of one variable over another. Over a period of time.It seemed redundant to view scatter plot for same set of characteristics. Hence I planned a separate drop down to select years and energy to observe the trends in consumption and production across various countries

    Trends Pointed out by the Visualization

    The following trends can be clearly observed using the above visualization:

    • Coal consumption increased during the 80’s decade which resulted in search of new coal mines and hence corresponding increase in production of coal during the 90’s to meet the demand across the world.
    • Petroleum consumption can be seen increasing constantly more steeply for the developing nations and less steeply for developed countries
    • The consumption of electricity can be seen increasing drastically while the generation of electricity falling behind.
    • Also to provide feedback on what countries the user is going to view, the names of selected countries are displayed alongside the choice button. The same are displayed using graph legends.
    • In short, we need more energy than we did back in 1980’s while we aren’t producing it much.
